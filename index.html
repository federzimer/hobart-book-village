Hobart is on State Route 10 in Delaware County, between Bloomville and Stamford in the Great Western Catskills. About 3 hours from NYC and 45 minutes from Oneonta. The entire village is walkable — park once and explore on foot.

Our Story

A Village Reborn
Through Books

From indigenous hunting grounds to a literary destination — the story of Hobart is one of reinvention, community, and the enduring power of a good book.

Pre-1763
Ancient Roots
Long before European settlement, the land around present-day Hobart served as hunting grounds for the Lenape and Mohawk peoples of the Northeastern Woodlands.
1784
Settlers Arrive
After the American Revolution, settlers — many from Connecticut and New England — moved into the area, building farms, mills, and inns. The village was originally known as Waterville.
1828
A New Name
The U.S. Postal Service requested the village rename itself to avoid confusion with the more populated Waterville, New York. The community became Hobart.
1884
The Railroad Arrives
A railroad line connected to the village, fueling growth through agriculture and commerce. Hobart thrived as a small Catskill Mountain community.
Late 1900s
A Village in Decline
Like much of rural upstate New York, Hobart saw businesses close, revenue decline, and population shrink through the second half of the twentieth century. Main Street grew quiet.
Early 2000s
Don Dales Sees a Vision
Don Dales, who grew up in nearby Stamford just three miles away, came to Hobart and saw a Main Street so empty you could imagine tumbleweeds blowing through. He bought up vacant buildings along Main Street with a bold idea: fill them with booksellers.
2001
The First Booksellers Arrive
Bill & Diana Adams — retired and dreaming of becoming booksellers — drove through Hobart from Manhattan, saw a corner store for rent, and took it on the spot. They began moving in books Bill already owned, establishing what would become Wm. H. Adams' Antiquarian Books.

Inspired by the legendary Book Village of Hay-on-Wye in Wales — where a single visionary turned a small town into a destination of nearly 25 bookstores — Don Dales began welcoming fellow entrepreneurs to open shops in his empty buildings, dreaming of a literature lover's enclave in the Catskills.

— The founding idea behind Hobart Book Village
2005
Hobart Book Village Is Born
Don Dales officially established Hobart Book Village — the only book village east of the Mississippi. By this point there were four bookstores, and the vision was taking shape. Dales welcomed more entrepreneurs to open shops alongside the growing literary community.
2009
Blenheim Hill Books Opens
Retired professors Barbara Balliet & Cheryl Clarke had bought a house near the village in 2005. After years of browsing and befriending booksellers, they jumped into the business themselves — opening Blenheim Hill Books and later co-founding the Hobart Festival of Women Writers.
2016–2020
New Shops & New Energy
Kathy & George Duyer opened Creative Corner Books in 2016, followed by New York Books & Ephemera in 2020. Liberty Rock Books — established in 1976 in Rockland County — relocated to Hobart, bringing 85,000 volumes. The village grew to seven independent shops.
Present
The Book Village Inn & Beyond
The Book Village Inn & Bar opened its doors at 760 Main Street, adding cozy accommodations and a gathering spot for visitors. Annual events like the Memorial Day and Thanksgiving Weekend Sales draw collectors from across the Northeast.
2026
The Next Chapter
A new bookshop and coffee bar is opening inside the Inn, and two existing shops are changing hands — proving that Don Dales' belief was right all along: books can bring a community back to life.

Dales didn't start Hobart Book Village to get rich. He started it to make a difference and build something that could last — a place that could help a community, create experiences, and bring people together.

— On Don Dales' legacy
